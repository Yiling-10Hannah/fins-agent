# Academic Writing Rules

Use these rules for all student reports, presentations, and written analysis.
They apply to Word-first reports and to legacy LaTeX only when LaTeX is
explicitly requested.

## Core Standard

- Lead with the finding. The first sentence should state a concrete result,
  not announce a topic.
- Use active voice and present tense for results: "Table 2 shows..." and
  "We estimate...".
- Prefer short, direct words. Use "use" instead of "utilize" and "many"
  instead of "myriad".
- State magnitudes. Replace vague claims such as "large" or "important" with
  numbers when data are available.
- Delete throat-clearing: "it is important to note", "this section discusses",
  "as discussed above", and similar meta-commentary.
- Avoid self-praise. Do not call the analysis "comprehensive", "novel",
  "striking", or "important"; show the evidence.
- Clothe "this" with a noun: "This result shows..." rather than "This shows...".
- Use uncertainty directly. If a claim needs checking, write
  `[HUMAN EDIT REQUIRED: verify ...]`.

## Structure

- Put the main contribution in the introduction.
- Each paragraph should advance one claim and support it with evidence.
- Avoid section-ending summary paragraphs unless the section is the conclusion.
- Keep old information before new information so sentences chain naturally.
- Vary sentence length and paragraph openings.

## Pre-Final Checklist

- No hard-banned words from `banned-words.md`.
- No unresolved placeholders, TODOs, or `[HUMAN EDIT REQUIRED]` markers.
- Every table and figure is referenced in the text.
- Quantitative claims include units, sample period, and magnitude when known.
- Citations are verified under `citation-verification.md`.
- Word reports also satisfy `word-reporting.md`.
